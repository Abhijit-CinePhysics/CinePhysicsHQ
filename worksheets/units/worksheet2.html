Worksheet 2

Class 11 Physics Practice Worksheet

Chapter: Units and Measurements Difficulty Level: Mixed (CBSE & NCERT aligned)

Learning Outcomes

  1. Apply the rules of significant figures to scientific measurements and calculations.
  2. Distinguish between absolute, relative, and percentage errors in physical quantities.
  3. Propagate uncertainties and errors through arithmetic operations to state final experimental results.

Instructions

  1. Answer all questions carefully using proper SI units and formatting.
  2. Pay strict attention to significant figures in your final numerical calculations.
  3. Show clear step-by-step propagation of errors in the numerical and short answer sections.

Section A: Multiple Choice Questions

  1. The number of significant figures in the measured values 0.007 m2, 2.64 × 1024 kg, and 0.2370 g cm-3 are respectively:
    1. 1, 3, and 4
    2. 3, 3, and 4
    3. 1, 3, and 3
    4. 4, 4, and 4
  2. A student measures the diameter of a wire using a screw gauge. The readings obtained are 1.25 mm, 1.26 mm, 1.24 mm, and 1.25 mm. The mean absolute error in the measurement is:
    1. 0.01 mm
    2. 0.005 mm
    3. 0.0075 mm
    4. 0.02 mm
  3. The mass and volume of a body are measured as 22.42 g and 4.7 cm3. The density of the body up to correct significant figures is:
    1. 4.8 g cm-3
    2. 4.77 g cm-3
    3. 4.770 g cm-3
    4. 4.7702 g cm-3
  4. Two physical quantities are measured as A = 2.0 m ± 0.2 m and B = 5.0 m ± 0.1 m. The value of (A + B) with uncertainty boundaries is:
    1. 7.0 m ± 0.3 m
    2. 7.0 m ± 0.1 m
    3. 7.0 m ± 0.2 m
  5. The length of a simple pendulum is measured with an accuracy of 1% and its time period is measured with an accuracy of 2%. The maximum percentage error in the determination of acceleration due to gravity (g) is:
    1. 5%
    2. 3%
    3. 4%
    4. 2%
  6. A distance is reported as 4.00 × 103 m. If this value is converted to kilometers, the number of significant figures in the result will be:
    1. 3
    2. 1
    3. 2
    4. 4
  7. The value of 15.647 minus 12.2 is calculated. Rounding off the answer to the correct number of significant figures gives:
    1. 3.4
    2. 3.45
    3. 3.447
    4. 3.5
  8. The relative error in a physical quantity Z which depends on independent variables a and b as Z = a3 b2 is given by the relation:
    1. ΔZ/Z = 3(Δa/a) + 2(Δb/b)
    2. ΔZ/Z = 3(Δa/a) - 2(Δb/b)
    3. ΔZ/Z = (Δa/a)3 + (Δb/b)2
    4. ΔZ/Z = 3(Δa) + 2(Δb)
  9. While measuring the time period of an oscillation, a stopwatch has a least count of 0.1 s. If the total time for 20 oscillations is 40.0 s, the percentage error in the measurement of time is:
    1. 0.25%
    2. 0.5%
    3. 0.1%
    4. 1.0%
  10. The absolute uncertainty in a measurement represents:
    1. The magnitude of the difference between the true value and the individual measured value.
    2. The ratio of the mean absolute error to the mean value.
    3. The percentage deviation from the standard theoretical value.
    4. The precision of the instrument expressed as a fraction of the total measurement.

Section B: Assertion Reason

  1. Assertion: When two physical quantities are multiplied, the relative error in the result is the sum of the relative errors in the individual quantities. Reason: The rules for propagation of errors ensure that maximum possible error is estimated in experimental measurements.
  2. Assertion: The value 5.0 m has the same level of uncertainty as the value 5.00 m. Reason: Trailing zeros after a decimal point are counted as significant figures.

Section C: Very Short Answer

  1. State the rule for determining the number of significant figures when rounding off the product of two physical quantities.
  2. Why does the process of measuring a physical quantity multiple times reduce the random error in the final mean result?

Section D: Short Answer

  1. A specific physical parameter is governed by the relation X = a b2 c-1. Derive an expression for the maximum fractional error in X when the individual measurements of a, b, and c contain absolute errors denoted by Δa, Δb, and Δc.
  2. Explain the fundamental differences between systematic errors and random errors. Provide one method to minimize each type during laboratory measurements.
  3. An experimenter records a series of length measurements using a vernier caliper with a least count of 0.01 cm. Explain how this least count defines the absolute uncertainty of a single measurement, and contrast this with the uncertainty derived from taking the average of ten measurements.

Section E: Numericals

  1. The length, breadth, and thickness of a rectangular sheet of metal are 4.234 m, 1.005 m, and 2.01 cm respectively. Calculate the total surface area and the volume of the sheet to the correct number of significant figures.
  2. An experimental value for the acceleration due to gravity is determined by measuring the length of a pendulum line and its period of oscillation. If the measured length L = 100.0 cm ± 0.1 cm and the time period T = 2.00 s ± 0.02 s, find the maximum percentage error in the calculated value of g.
  3. The initial and final temperatures of a liquid column are recorded as θ1 = 18.0 °C ± 0.5 °C and θ2 = 40.0 °C ± 0.3 °C. Compute the rise in temperature of the liquid with its proper uncertainty boundary.
  4. The resistance R of a conductor is determined using the relation R = V / I. If V = 100 V ± 5 V and I = 10 A ± 0.2 A, calculate the value of R along with its percentage error and express the final result with appropriate uncertainty.
  5. The side of a cube is measured to be 7.203 m. Calculate the total surface area and the volume of the cube to appropriate significant figures.
  6. A physical quantity P is calculated from the expression P = a3 b2 / (c d1/2). If the percentage errors of measurement in a, b, c, and d are 1%, 3%, 4%, and 2% respectively, calculate the maximum percentage error possible in the physical quantity P.
  7. The diameter of a spherical ball is measured to be 2.48 cm using a screw gauge. If the absolute error in the diameter measurement is ± 0.02 cm, calculate the volume of the sphere and state its associated absolute uncertainty.
  8. Three measurements of a time interval are recorded as 12.12 s, 12.15 s, and 12.14 s. Calculate the mean value, the mean absolute error, and express the final time interval with its uncertainty up to the correct number of significant figures.
  9. An object travels a displacement Δx = 150.0 m ± 0.5 m in a time interval of 10.0 s ± 0.2 s. Determine the average velocity of the object and calculate the absolute uncertainty in this calculated velocity value.
  10. A laboratory standard resistor of value 50.0 Ω ± 0.5 Ω is connected in series with another resistor of value 100.0 Ω ± 0.8 Ω. Find the equivalent resistance of the series combination and express the final result with its absolute error boundary.
